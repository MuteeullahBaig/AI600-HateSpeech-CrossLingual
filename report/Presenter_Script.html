Slide 2  ·  Problem Statement  ·  Presenter A
~1.5 minutes
Say — top-left box: Why Nastaliq Urdu?
Urdu is spoken by 230 million people and is the dominant informal language on Pakistani social media, yet hate speech detection tools are almost entirely built for English. We use the ISE-Hate corpus — 21,759 annotated Nastaliq Urdu tweets from Akram et al. 2023 — the first large-scale hate detection dataset for this language. Crucially, Nastaliq Urdu is already in XLM-R's 100-language pre-training. Roman Urdu — written in Latin letters — is not. So our choice of script directly affects whether the model has any representations to start from.
Say — top-right box: Study Overview
In brief: we fine-tune multilingual transformers on English hate speech data, evaluate how well they transfer to Nastaliq Urdu under zero-shot and few-shot conditions, and compare those results against classical machine learning baselines.
Say — Research Questions
We structured our work around four research questions.
Point to RQ1
RQ1: Does model capacity matter? We test mBERT at 179 million parameters, XLM-R-base at 278 million, and XLM-R-large at 560 million. Spoiler: bigger is better, even for zero-shot Urdu.
Point to RQ2
RQ2: Is zero-shot failure caused by the English training data being class-imbalanced — 94.8% non-hate — or by genuine domain incompatibility? The answer tells us whether we have a calibration problem or a transfer problem.
Point to RQ3
RQ3: Do K-shot examples from Urdu enable stable, useful transfer? On some tasks, few-shot fine-tuning collapses. We wanted to know if Nastaliq Urdu is one of those cases.
Point to RQ4
And RQ4: Does the model treat all types of hate speech fairly? ISE-Hate has four Level-2 sub-categories — Interfaith, Sectarian, Ethnic, Other — and we measure whether the model's blind spots fall on specific groups.

Slide 3  ·  Dataset Description  ·  Presenter A
~1.5 minutes
Say — SOURCE column
Our source language dataset is from Davidson et al. 2017. It contains about 24,000 English tweets — we split into 19,826 train, 2,477 dev, and 2,477 test. Only 5.2% are labelled hate speech — the rest are offensive or neither. This severe class imbalance has consequences we will discuss in a moment.
Say — TARGET column
Our target dataset is ISE-Hate by Akram et al. 2023 — 21,759 Nastaliq Urdu tweets collected from Twitter, annotated by native speakers. We use 15,231 for training, 2,176 for development, and 4,352 for the final test set. Hate speech is 39.8% of the data — far more balanced than English. Inter-annotator agreement is Cohen's κ equals 0.71 — that is substantial agreement, indicating the labels are reliable. The dataset also provides Level-2 sub-category labels: Interfaith, Sectarian, Ethnic, and Other.
Point to the amber key challenge callout at the bottom
Here is the critical mismatch. The English model learns that 94.8% of tweets are non-hate — it develops a very strong prior toward predicting non-hate. When we apply that model to Urdu, where 39.8% of tweets actually are hate, the model is badly miscalibrated before it sees a single Urdu word. This is the root cause of zero-shot failure — a calibration problem, not a language coverage problem.
If asked why not use a balanced English dataset: Davidson 2017 is the canonical benchmark, used by most cross-lingual comparison papers. Using it keeps our results comparable to the literature. The imbalance is the research challenge, not an error in our design.

Slide 4  ·  Methodology  ·  Presenter B
~2 minutes
Say — model cards
We tested three multilingual transformer models. All are pre-trained on text from 100 languages, all include Nastaliq Urdu in their vocabulary. mBERT — 179 million parameters — serves as our baseline. XLM-R-base — 278 million — for ablations. And our primary model is XLM-R-large — 560 million parameters — pre-trained by Meta on 2.5 terabytes of web text with temperature-based language sampling that gives Urdu stronger representations.
Point to protocol 1: Zero-Shot
Protocol 1 — Zero-shot: we fine-tune on English only, then run the checkpoint directly on the Urdu test set with no Urdu training at all. As we just explained, the model is miscalibrated from the English imbalance — so recall drops to 0.080 while precision stays at 0.885. It rarely fires. The failure is low recall, not low precision.
Point to protocol 2: Few-Shot
Protocol 2 — Few-shot: we take K stratified Urdu examples — K of 8, 16, 32, or 64 — and continue fine-tuning from the English checkpoint. We calibrate the class weights to [1.0, 2.5] for the Urdu 40% hate rate — not the [1.0, 8.0] we used in English, which would massively over-weight hate.
Point to protocol 3: Classical ML
Protocol 3 — Classical ML: unigram and bigram TF-IDF features with 50,000-feature vocabulary, combined with Logistic Regression or Random Forest. We also tested FuzzyWuzzy string-matching features against a hate-phrase lexicon. These train on the full 15,231 Urdu training samples.
Point to protocol 4: Supervised
Protocol 4 — Supervised fine-tuning: XLM-R-large fine-tuned on all 15,231 Urdu examples, starting from the English checkpoint. This is our upper bound — how good can we get if we have full Urdu supervision?
If asked about gradient checkpointing: trading 30% compute for ~30% GPU memory savings — required to fit XLM-R-large on our 12 GB RTX 4070. Without it, the 560M parameter model would OOM at batch size 8.

Slide 5  ·  Results & Comparison  ·  Presenter B
~2.5 minutes
Say — introduce metrics
Our primary metrics are Hate F1 — how well we catch hate speech — Macro F1 — the average across both classes, which penalises ignoring either — and FPR, the false positive rate: how often safe content is wrongly flagged as hate. All results are on the ISE-Hate test set of 4,352 Nastaliq Urdu tweets.
Point to zero-shot row
Zero-shot: hate F1 of 0.147, macro F1 of 0.462. The FPR is only 0.046 — the model almost never fires at all. This is the class-imbalance prior at work. Precision is 0.885 — nearly perfect — but recall is 0.080. The model knows what English hate looks like; it just almost never sees those patterns in Urdu. And notice: XLM-R-large is the best at zero-shot among the three backbones — this is not negative transfer. Larger means better Urdu representations.
Point to few-shot rows (K=8 through K=64)
With just 8 Urdu examples, hate F1 jumps to 0.590. It rises monotonically: K equals 16 gives 0.619, K equals 32 gives 0.649, K equals 64 gives 0.694. No collapse at any K. Note that the FPR also rises — from 0.046 to 0.205 — as the model learns to fire more. There is a fundamental trade-off between catching hate and over-flagging neutral content.
Point to TF-IDF+LR row
The classical baseline trained on all 15,231 Urdu examples achieves hate F1 of 0.747, outperforming our best few-shot result by 5.3 points. Critically, FuzzyWuzzy adds exactly zero benefit — the hate-phrase lexicon is in Latin script, ISE-Hate is in Arabic script. String matching across scripts produces near-zero similarity scores for every tweet. Pure noise. This is a script mismatch that would not occur with Roman Urdu.
Point to supervised XLM-R-large row (highlighted)
The supervised upper bound: hate F1 of 0.796 and macro F1 of 0.816. But the FPR is 0.228 — higher than TF-IDF+LR at 0.154. The neural model gains 0.049 hate F1 but produces 48% more false positives. The dominant signal in ISE-Hate is lexical. For a use case where over-censoring is the bigger concern, TF-IDF is the better choice.
Point to the three callout boxes at the bottom
The three callouts summarise our trajectory: 0.147 zero-shot — a failure. 0.747 TF-IDF — beats all few-shot neural methods. 0.796 supervised — our project best.

Slide 6  ·  Key Findings  ·  Presenter B
~2 minutes
Say — introduce
Let me summarise our five key findings.
Point to Finding 1
Finding 1: zero-shot fails due to class-imbalance prior — not domain gap. The English training data is 94.8% non-hate, so the model almost never predicts hate. Recall equals 0.080, precision equals 0.885. It is a calibration failure. And XLM-R-large is the best zero-shot model — no negative transfer.
Point to Finding 2
Finding 2: few-shot transfer is stable and monotonically improving. Hate F1 rises from 0.590 at K equals 8 to 0.694 at K equals 64. No collapse on Nastaliq Urdu. Correct class weights are key — [1.0, 2.5] for 40% hate, not [1.0, 8.0] from English.
Point to Finding 3
Finding 3: classical ML beats all few-shot neural methods. TF-IDF plus Logistic Regression on full data achieves hate F1 of 0.747. FuzzyWuzzy adds zero benefit — Latin lexicon versus Arabic Nastaliq script is a complete mismatch. Random Forest achieves the lowest false positive rate, 0.056.
Point to Finding 4
Finding 4: the supervised XLM-R-large achieves strong performance at a high FPR cost. Hate F1 of 0.796 — that is +0.049 over TF-IDF — but FPR rises from 0.154 to 0.228. The dominant signal is lexical. 560 times more compute for a 4.9-point gain may not be justified in a deployment setting.
Point to Finding 5
Finding 5: there is a twenty-times zero-shot recall disparity across hate sub-categories. Interfaith hate recall is 28.2% — Other hate recall is just 1.4%. The English annotators — Amazon Mechanical Turk workers, predominantly US-based — were more sensitive to religious-adjacent language and international-facing slurs. When transferred to Urdu, this bias means the model catches English-pattern hate while being nearly blind to native-Urdu-idiom hate speech. The "Other" category, the largest with 1,100 test samples, is written in culturally specific Pakistani idiom with no English surface form — invisible to the model.
Say — closing
To summarise: cross-lingual hate speech transfer to Nastaliq Urdu is hard. Zero-shot fails on calibration, not language coverage. Few-shot adapts stably with the correct class weights. But annotator bias from English training creates systematic blind spots in low-resource groups that no amount of cross-lingual transfer can fully overcome without native-language labelled data. Thank you — we are happy to take your questions.
End with a confident pause. Do not say "that's all" or "I think that's it" — end on the word "questions."
Q&A — Quick Answers (30–45 seconds each)
10 minutes
If asked why zero-shot fails
The English training data is 94.8% non-hate. The model develops a strong "predict non-hate" prior. At zero-shot, precision is 0.885 — nearly perfect — but recall is 0.080. The model knows what hate looks like; it just almost never sees English-style hate signals in Urdu text.
If asked why the larger model is better at zero-shot Urdu
XLM-R-large has better Urdu representations: larger vocabulary (250K vs 119K for mBERT), more web training data, and temperature-based language sampling. The capacity ranking on English is preserved on zero-shot Urdu. This is not negative transfer — better language coverage beats stronger source-domain calibration.
If asked why no few-shot collapse
Two reasons: class weights [1.0, 2.5] calibrated for the Urdu 40% hate rate — not English [1.0, 8.0] which would massively over-weight hate on balanced data. And Nastaliq Urdu is in XLM-R's pre-training, so the model already has meaningful Urdu representations that provide real gradient signals from step one.
If asked why FuzzyWuzzy did nothing
FuzzyWuzzy matches tweet text against a Latin-script lexicon. ISE-Hate is Nastaliq Arabic script. String matching across scripts produces near-zero similarity for every tweet — the features are pure noise. This would work on Roman Urdu, where lexicon and text share Latin characters.
If asked why Macro F1 and not accuracy
A model that predicts everything as non-hate gets 60% accuracy on ISE-Hate and catches zero hate speech. Macro F1 averages F1 across both classes — failing either class hurts equally.
If asked about supervised vs TF-IDF trade-off
Supervised XLM-R-large: hate F1 equals 0.796, FPR equals 0.228. TF-IDF plus LR: hate F1 equals 0.747, FPR equals 0.154. The neural model gains 0.049 hate F1 but produces 48% more false positives. For a deployment where over-censoring is the bigger concern, TF-IDF is the better choice.
If asked about the 20× fairness disparity
At zero-shot, Interfaith recall is 28.2% while Other hate recall is 1.4% — a twenty-times gap. The English training data biased the model toward hate patterns it has seen before: religious-adjacent hate with English surface forms. Native-Urdu-idiom hate speech in the "Other" category is completely invisible to the zero-shot model.
If asked what you would do differently
Three things: build a Nastaliq-script hate lexicon so string-matching features work; extend few-shot to larger K values to find the point where neural catches TF-IDF; and investigate adapter-based fine-tuning for more stable adaptation at small K.
