Slide 2  ·  Problem Statement & Dataset  ·  Presenter A
~2 minutes
Say — Why this is hard
Hate speech detection tools are almost entirely built for English. Urdu, spoken by over 230 million people, is severely under-resourced for this task. Our goal is to understand how much an English-trained model can help — and where it fails — when applied to Pakistani Urdu hate speech.
Say — Research Questions
We structured our work around four research questions.
Point to RQ1
RQ1: Does model size matter? We tested mBERT, XLM-R-base, and XLM-R-large — models of increasing size. Spoiler: yes, bigger is better, even for Urdu.
Point to RQ2
RQ2: Can an English-trained model detect Urdu hate speech with zero Urdu examples? This is zero-shot transfer. Short answer: it fails — but in an interesting and informative way.
Point to RQ3
RQ3: If we give the model 8, 16, 32, or 64 labelled Urdu tweets, does it improve? Unlike some transfer settings, here it does — steadily and stably.
Point to RQ4
And RQ4: Does the model treat all types of hate speech fairly? We used ISE-Hate's Level-2 sub-categories — Interfaith, Sectarian, Ethnic, Other — to measure whether the model is systematically blind to any group.
Say — Dataset
Our source data is the Davidson et al. 2017 English dataset: about 21,000 training samples, but only 5.2% are hate speech. Our target is ISE-Hate: 15,231 Urdu training tweets, with 39.8% hate. This mismatch — 5% hate in English versus 40% in Urdu — is the root cause of zero-shot failure, as I will explain shortly.
If asked "why not just translate Urdu to English?": Translation is lossy for hate speech — culturally specific slurs, sectarian terminology, and idiomatic insults either don't translate or translate incorrectly. Our approach works directly on the raw Urdu text.
Move to Slide 3
Slide 3  ·  Methodology  ·  Presenter A
~2 minutes
Say — Models
We tested three multilingual transformer models, all pre-trained on text from 100 languages including Urdu. mBERT is our smallest baseline at 179 million parameters. XLM-R-base for ablation at 278 million. And our primary model is XLM-R-large at 560 million parameters, trained by Meta on 2.5 terabytes of web text. Importantly, Nastaliq Urdu is in XLM-R's vocabulary — so the model already understands the script before we fine-tune it.
Say — English training
Phase one: we fine-tune all three models on the English dataset. With only 5.2% hate samples, we use class-weighted loss to prevent the model from ignoring hate speech entirely — missing a hate sample costs 8 times more than missing a non-hate sample.
Point to Transfer Protocols
Say — Transfer protocols
We then apply three transfer protocols. Zero-shot: apply the English model directly to Urdu tweets — no Urdu training. Few-shot: give the model K labelled Urdu examples — 8, 16, 32, or 64 — and continue fine-tuning with class weights calibrated for Urdu's 40% hate rate. We run each K with three random seeds to measure stability. And the supervised upper bound: train on all 15,231 Urdu examples to see what is achievable with full supervision.
Say — Classical ML
We also run classical machine learning baselines: TF-IDF features combined with Logistic Regression or Random Forest, with and without FuzzyWuzzy string-matching features. These are trained on the full Urdu training set and serve as a practical benchmark.
If asked about gradient checkpointing: it is a memory-saving technique — recomputing activations during backpropagation instead of storing them, trading 30% speed for 30% less GPU memory. Required for XLM-R-large on our 12 GB GPU.

Slide 4  ·  Results  ·  Presenter B
~2.5 minutes
Say — introduce metrics
Our primary metrics are Hate F1 — how well we catch hate speech — Macro F1 — the average F1 across both classes, which penalises ignoring either — and FPR, the false positive rate: how often safe content is wrongly flagged as hate.
Point to English results table
On English, the capacity hierarchy is clear: XLM-R-large achieves the best hate F1 of 0.509 and the lowest FPR of 0.030. This ordering is important because it is preserved in zero-shot Urdu.
Point to zero-shot table
Zero-shot transfer: all models fail — hate F1 below 0.15. But notice something important: XLM-R-large is still the best at zero-shot, at 0.147. This is not negative transfer. The failure mode is low recall, not low precision: precision is 0.885 — nearly perfect — but recall is 0.080. The model almost never predicts hate. It learned English hate patterns that simply don't appear in Urdu text, so it almost never fires.
Point to few-shot table
With few-shot adaptation, hate F1 rises monotonically: 0.590 at K equals 8, all the way to 0.694 at K equals 64. No collapse, no degenerate behaviour. The K equals 16 variance is high — macro F1 drops to 0.544 — because 16 examples are sometimes an unlucky batch. By K equals 64, the model is completely stable: variance essentially zero.
Point to classical ML and supervised rows
The classical TF-IDF plus Logistic Regression baseline, trained on all 15,231 Urdu examples, achieves hate F1 of 0.747 — outperforming our best few-shot result by 5 points. Interestingly, FuzzyWuzzy adds exactly zero benefit — its hate-phrase lexicon is in Latin script, and ISE-Hate is in Arabic script. String matching across scripts produces pure noise. The supervised XLM-R-large reaches 0.796 hate F1 and 0.816 macro F1, but with a higher false positive rate of 0.228 versus 0.154 for TF-IDF.
Key talking point: the 5.3 point gap between K=64 (0.694) and TF-IDF+LR (0.747) represents the cost of 64 labelled examples versus 15,231. This is the cross-lingual transfer gap quantified.

Slide 5  ·  Fairness & Discussion  ·  Presenter B
~2 minutes
Say — introduce fairness
Beyond overall metrics, we analysed how the model performs across different types of hate speech using ISE-Hate's Level-2 sub-categories.
Point to fairness table
At zero-shot, Interfaith hate speech recall is 28.2% — but Other hate recall is just 1.4%. That is a twenty-times disparity. Interfaith hate speech sometimes uses terms the English-trained model recognises — transliterated religious terms, internationally used slurs. But the "Other" category — which is the largest, with 1,100 test samples — is written in native Urdu idiom with no English surface form. The model is essentially completely blind to it.
Say — annotator bias
This disparity traces back to the English training data. The Davidson dataset was annotated by Amazon Mechanical Turk workers — predominantly white and US-based. Research has shown they over-labelled African American dialect features as hate speech and were more sensitive to religious language. When transferred to Urdu, this bias means the model fires on content that resembles English hate patterns while missing culturally specific Pakistani hate in native Urdu.
Say — few-shot improvement
The good news: at K equals 64, few-shot adaptation nearly equalises the structured categories. Interfaith recall reaches 98.7%, Sectarian 94.8%, Ethnic 94.1%. The "Other" category is the hardest — still only 71.2% recall — because it is the most lexically diverse and idiomatic. But the false positive rate jumps from 0.7% to 31.7% as we gain recall. There is a fundamental trade-off between catching hate and over-flagging neutral content.
If asked about the FPR jump: as the model learns to detect interfaith and sectarian hate, it also flags interfaith and sectarian neutral content. A user discussing religion without hate intent faces a 31.7% chance of being flagged. This is a real deployment concern for Pakistani content moderation.

Slide 6  ·  Key Findings & Conclusion  ·  Presenter B
~1.5 minutes
Say — introduce
Let me summarise our five key findings.
Point to Finding 1
First: zero-shot transfer fails, but not due to language coverage. Urdu is in XLM-R's pre-training — the failure is due to class-imbalance bias from English training, causing near-zero recall. The larger model is still the best — no negative transfer here.
Point to Finding 2
Second: choosing the correct target language prevents few-shot collapse. Nastaliq Urdu, being in XLM-R's vocabulary, allows stable monotonic improvement from 0.590 at K equals 8 to 0.694 at K equals 64.
Point to Finding 3
Third: there is a 20-times zero-shot recall disparity across hate sub-categories. Annotator bias from the English source propagates cross-lingually, systematically blinding the model to native-Urdu idiom hate speech.
Point to Finding 4
Fourth: TF-IDF plus Logistic Regression is a strong fully-supervised baseline at hate F1 of 0.747, outperforming all few-shot neural methods. FuzzyWuzzy adds nothing — script mismatch kills lexical matching.
Point to Finding 5
Fifth: supervised XLM-R-large achieves 0.796 hate F1, modestly beating TF-IDF but with significantly higher false positives. The dominant signal in ISE-Hate is lexical — deep multilingual representations add modest value at real cost.
Say — closing
To summarise: cross-lingual hate speech transfer to Urdu is genuinely hard. Zero-shot fails due to calibration, not language coverage. Few-shot adaptation works stably with the correct class weights. But annotator bias from English training creates systematic blind spots that no amount of cross-lingual transfer can fully overcome without native-language labelled data. Thank you. We are happy to take your questions.

Q&A — Quick Answers (30–45 seconds each)
10 minutes
If asked why zero-shot fails
The English training data is 94.8% non-hate. The model develops a strong "predict non-hate" prior. At zero-shot, precision is 0.885 — nearly perfect — but recall is 0.080. The model knows what hate looks like; it just almost never sees English-style hate signals in Urdu text.
If asked why larger model is better at zero-shot Urdu
XLM-R-large has better Urdu representations because of larger vocabulary (250K vs 119K), more web training data, and temperature-based language sampling. The capacity ranking on English is preserved on zero-shot Urdu. This is not negative transfer — it is a case where better language coverage beats stronger source-domain calibration.
If asked why no collapse in few-shot
Two reasons: we use class weights [1.0, 2.5] calibrated for the Urdu 40% hate rate — not the English [1.0, 8.0] which would massively over-weight hate on balanced data. And Nastaliq Urdu is in XLM-R's pre-training, so the model already has meaningful Urdu representations that give real gradient signals from step one.
If asked why FuzzyWuzzy did nothing
FuzzyWuzzy matches tweet text against a Latin-script lexicon. ISE-Hate is Nastaliq Arabic script. String matching across scripts produces near-zero similarity for every tweet — the features are pure noise. This would work on Roman Urdu, where the lexicon and text share Latin characters.
If asked why Macro F1 and not accuracy
A model that predicts everything as non-hate gets 60% accuracy on ISE-Hate and catches zero hate speech. Macro F1 averages F1 across both classes — failing either class hurts the score equally.
If asked about the supervised vs TF-IDF trade-off
Supervised XLM-R-large: hate F1 = 0.796, FPR = 0.228. TF-IDF+LR: hate F1 = 0.747, FPR = 0.154. The neural model gains 0.049 hate F1 but produces 48% more false positives. For a deployment where over-censoring is the bigger concern, TF-IDF is the better choice. For maximising hate recall, the neural model is better.
If asked about the fairness finding
At zero-shot, Interfaith recall is 28.2% while Other hate recall is 1.4% — a 20x disparity. The English training data biased the model toward hate patterns it has seen before: religious-adjacent hate with English surface forms. Native-Urdu-idiom hate speech is completely invisible to the zero-shot model.
If asked what you would do differently
Three things: build a Nastaliq-script hate lexicon so string-matching features actually work; extend few-shot to larger K values to find when neural catches TF-IDF; and investigate adapter-based fine-tuning for more stable adaptation at small K.
