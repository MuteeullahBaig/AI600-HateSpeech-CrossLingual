Slide 1
Cross-Lingual Hate Speech Detection: English → Nastaliq Urdu Transfer Learning

What this project is about

This project asks a fundamental question: can a model trained to detect hate speech in English automatically work on Nastaliq Urdu — Pakistani Twitter — without needing thousands of labelled Urdu examples? This is called cross-lingual transfer learning.

What is Nastaliq Urdu?

Nastaliq is the Arabic-script writing system used for formal and social-media Urdu in Pakistan. Unlike Roman Urdu (Urdu typed with Latin letters), Nastaliq Urdu is a standardised script and is one of the 100 languages in XLM-RoBERTa's pre-training corpus. This makes it a methodologically sound transfer target — the model has seen the script, just never in the context of hate speech detection.

The ISE-Hate Dataset

We use the ISE-Hate corpus (Akram et al., 2023): 21,759 Pakistani tweets annotated by five bilingual native speakers. The dataset is unique in providing two annotation levels: Level-1 (binary hate/neutral) and Level-2 (sub-categories: Interfaith, Sectarian, Ethnic, Other). Cohen's κ = 0.71 — substantial agreement.

Why Nastaliq Urdu and not Roman Urdu?
Nastaliq Urdu is in XLM-R's pre-training data, making it a proper test of cross-lingual transfer within the model's known linguistic space. Roman Urdu uses Latin script and is mostly out-of-vocabulary for XLM-R — failures there conflate script coverage with transfer quality. Nastaliq Urdu isolates the cultural/domain gap from the script gap, giving us cleaner experimental conclusions.
Why not just use GPT-4?
Our research goal is to study cross-lingual transfer in a low-resource, few-shot setting using multilingual transformers. LLMs are expensive to run locally and raise data privacy concerns. Our approach (XLM-R fine-tuning) runs on a single consumer GPU (RTX 4070) and is deployable at scale in production content moderation systems.
Slide 2
Problem Statement

Why Nastaliq Urdu? (top-left box on slide)

  • Arabic-script Urdu spoken by 230 million people — severely under-resourced for hate speech detection
  • ISE-Hate: first large annotated Urdu hate corpus (21,759 tweets, Akram et al. 2023)
  • Nastaliq in XLM-R's 100-language pre-training — Roman Urdu is not (out-of-vocabulary Latin script)

Study Overview (top-right box on slide)

  • Fine-tune multilingual transformers on English hate speech data (Davidson 2017)
  • Evaluate zero-shot & few-shot transfer to Nastaliq Urdu (ISE-Hate)
  • Compare neural cross-lingual transfer vs. classical ML baselines (TF-IDF+LR/RF)

Four Research Questions (RQ rows on slide)

  • RQ1 (Backbone): How does model capacity (mBERT → XLM-R-base → XLM-R-large) affect English performance and Urdu zero-shot transfer?
  • RQ2 (Zero-shot failure): Is zero-shot failure due to class-imbalance prior (calibration) or genuine domain gap?
  • RQ3 (Few-shot stability): Do K-shot Urdu examples enable stable, useful few-shot transfer without collapse?
  • RQ4 (Fairness): Does the model exhibit differential recall/FPR across the Level-2 hate sub-categories (Interfaith, Sectarian, Ethnic, Other)?
Key framing: The research questions are structured to distinguish calibration failure (RQ2) from domain failure. The answer — calibration dominates — has clear practical implications: it means few-shot adaptation (RQ3) is sufficient to recover most of the performance gap without needing full supervision.
Why is RQ2 (calibration vs domain gap) important?
If the failure is domain gap (the model cannot understand Urdu hate content), the only fix is collecting large amounts of labelled Urdu data. If the failure is calibration (the model's decision threshold is wrong due to the English class imbalance), then adjusting class weights and providing a small amount of Urdu data (few-shot) is sufficient. Our findings show calibration dominates — the practical implication is that ISE-Hate hate detection can be bootstrapped with just 64 labelled Urdu examples, not thousands.

Key challenge (amber callout on slide): EN training data is 94.8% non-hate → the model learns a strong non-hate prior. When applied to ISE-Hate (39.8% hate), the model is severely miscalibrated before seeing a single Urdu token. This is the root cause of zero-shot failure — a calibration problem, not a language coverage problem.

Annotation Quality Notes

ISE-Hate: Cohen's κ = 0.71, annotated by 5 bilingual Pakistani native speakers using majority vote. The Level-2 sub-categories were agreed upon by consensus among annotators.

Davidson 2017: ~30% of examples were contested between "hate speech" and "offensive language" labels. This annotation noise is baked into the English model's weights and propagates to Urdu transfer.

What are the ISE-Hate Level-2 sub-categories?
Level-2 sub-categorises the hate tweets: Interfaith (I) — inter-religious tension between different faiths; Sectarian (S) — intra-Islamic conflict (e.g. Sunni vs Shia); Ethnic (E) — ethnic minority hatred; Other (O) — generic hate not fitting the named categories. "Other" is the largest at n=1,100 in the test set and is the hardest to detect because it is lexically diverse and idiomatic rather than tied to specific recognisable terms.
Why does the 5.2% / 39.8% hate rate difference cause zero-shot failure?
The English model is calibrated by its training data to expect that only ~5% of tweets are hate. Even with class weights [1.0, 8.0] during English training, the model's internal representation of "what confidence is needed to predict hate" is set for a world where 94.8% of inputs are non-hate. When deployed on ISE-Hate — where 39.8% are hate — the threshold is far too conservative. The model almost never fires: recall = 0.080. The fix for few-shot training is to recalibrate the class weights to [1.0, 2.5] to match the Urdu 40/60 distribution.

Methodology

The Three Model Backbones (cards on slide)

Why XLM-R-large is primary: (1) 250K vocab covers more Nastaliq Urdu subwords than mBERT's 119K. (2) CommonCrawl web text includes informal social-media Urdu. (3) Temperature-based language sampling (T=0.7) oversamples low-resource languages including Urdu — ~3–5× more Urdu training data than naive proportional sampling. (4) 24 layers allow deeper compositional representations of culturally specific content.

English Fine-Tuning (not shown explicitly on slide but underpins all protocols)

  • Class-weighted loss [1.0, 8.0]: 5.2% hate → upweight hate 8×. Without this, the model collapses to always predicting non-hate.
  • LR 1×10⁻⁵ (large), 2×10⁻⁵ (others): small to preserve multilingual representations.
  • 5 epochs, select best by dev hate F1: prevents selecting on accuracy which ignores the hate class.
  • fp16 + gradient checkpointing (large only): stays within 12 GB VRAM on RTX 4070.

Protocol 1: Zero-Shot (item 1 on slide)

English checkpoint evaluated directly on ISE-Hate test set. No Urdu supervision at any stage. Failure mode: recall = 0.080, precision = 0.885 — model almost never fires.

Protocol 2: Few-Shot K=8/16/32/64 (item 2 on slide)

K stratified Urdu examples added to fine-tuning, starting from English checkpoint. Class weights recalibrated to [1.0, 2.5] for Urdu's 40/60 distribution. Dev set (2,176) used for checkpoint selection; test set evaluated exactly once at end. Run with 3 seeds to measure stability.

Protocol 3: Classical ML — TF-IDF+LR/RF (item 3 on slide)

TF-IDF (unigrams+bigrams, 50K features) combined with Logistic Regression or Random Forest. Also tested with FuzzyWuzzy string-matching features from a hate-phrase lexicon. Trained on full ISE-Hate train set (15,231 samples).

FuzzyWuzzy result: zero improvement. The hate-phrase lexicon is in Latin script. ISE-Hate is Arabic Nastaliq script. String similarity = near-zero for every tweet. Complete script mismatch.

Protocol 4: Supervised Fine-Tuning (item 4 on slide)

XLM-R-large fine-tuned on all 15,231 ISE-Hate train samples. Starting from English checkpoint. Class weights [1.0, 2.5]. 1 epoch (learning rate 1×10⁻⁵). This is the upper bound for fully supervised performance.

Why does few-shot NOT collapse, unlike some transfer settings?
Two key reasons: (1) Class weights are recalibrated to [1.0, 2.5] for Urdu's 40/60 distribution — not the [1.0, 8.0] used in English, which would massively over-predict hate on nearly-balanced data. (2) Nastaliq Urdu is in XLM-R's pre-training vocabulary — the model already has meaningful Urdu token representations that provide real gradient signal from step one. There is no "both strategies produce equal loss" degenerate minimum because the calibration is correct and the model already understands the script.
Why does FuzzyWuzzy have zero effect?
FuzzyWuzzy computes fuzzy string similarity between tweet text and a lexicon of known hate phrases. The lexicon is in Latin script (built for Roman Urdu / English). ISE-Hate is entirely in Nastaliq Arabic script. String matching between Arabic-script text and a Latin-script lexicon produces near-zero similarity scores on every tweet — the feature values are pure noise. This directly contrasts with Roman Urdu datasets where the lexicon and text share the same script and FuzzyWuzzy provides real signal.
How did you prevent test-set leakage?
The Urdu dev set (2,176 samples) is used for checkpoint selection — best hate F1 on dev determines the saved model. The test set (4,352 samples) is evaluated exactly once per experiment using zero_shot_eval.py, which is a separate script from train.py that takes only the checkpoint and test file. The test file is never passed to train.py at all.

Zero-shot failure mode is LOW RECALL, not low precision. XLM-R-large: precision = 0.885 (near-perfect) but recall = 0.080. FPR = 0.046 — the model almost never predicts hate. This is the class-imbalance prior at work, not domain incompatibility.
FPR–Recall trade-off: as K increases (0→8→64), both hate recall AND FPR increase. The model learns to fire more, catching more hate but also over-flagging more neutral content. Supervised XLM-R-large achieves the highest hate recall (0.890) but the highest FPR (0.228). RF achieves the lowest FPR (0.056) but at the cost of lower hate recall. There is no universally optimal point — deployment context determines the best operating point.
Why is supervised XLM-R-large's FPR higher than TF-IDF+LR?
The deep model uses broader contextual cues to raise recall, but this comes at the cost of more false positives — it fires on contextual patterns associated with hate in Urdu but not exclusively hateful (e.g. interfaith discussion in neutral context). TF-IDF+LR is more conservative: it fires on specific vocabulary that appears almost exclusively in hate tweets. For a deployment where false positives (over-censoring) are the bigger concern, RF (FPR=0.056) is the better choice despite lower recall.
Why does few-shot plateau at 0.694 while TF-IDF+LR reaches 0.747?
The 5.3-point gap quantifies the cost of 64 labelled examples vs 15,231. With 64 samples, XLM-R-large cannot fully learn the lexical distribution of Urdu hate speech — it relies on cross-lingual transfer from English representations which are imperfect for culturally specific Urdu content. TF-IDF+LR, trained on all 15,231 samples, directly observes the full Urdu hate vocabulary. The dominant signal in ISE-Hate is lexical; deep representations add modest incremental value when full supervision is available.
Slide 6
Key Findings

The slide shows 5 numbered finding cards on a dark navy background. Each card has a teal title and a one-to-two-sentence body. Know the precise numbers for every card.

Finding 1: Zero-shot fails due to class-imbalance prior — not domain gap

All models achieve hate F1 ≤ 0.147 at zero-shot. Failure mode is low recall (0.080), not low precision (0.885). XLM-R-large is the best at zero-shot — no negative transfer. Larger models transfer better because they have stronger Urdu representations.

Finding 2: Few-shot transfer is stable and monotonically improving

Hate F1 rises K=8 (0.590) → K=16 (0.619) → K=32 (0.649) → K=64 (0.694). No collapse at any K. Class weights [1.0, 2.5] calibrated to Urdu 40% hate rate (not [1.0, 8.0] from English). By K=64, variance drops to ±0.001.

Finding 3: Classical ML beats all few-shot neural methods

TF-IDF+LR on full data: hate F1 = 0.747. FuzzyWuzzy adds zero benefit — Latin lexicon vs Arabic-script Nastaliq = script mismatch. Random Forest achieves the lowest FPR (0.056) at the cost of lower hate recall.

Finding 4: Supervised XLM-R-large achieves strong performance at high FPR cost

Hate F1 = 0.796 (+0.049 over TF-IDF+LR), macro F1 = 0.816. FPR rises 0.154 → 0.228 (48% relative increase). Dominant signal is lexical. ~560× more compute than TF-IDF+LR for a 4.9-point hate F1 gain — may not be justified in a deployment where false positives are the primary concern.

Finding 5: 20× Level-2 zero-shot recall disparity (Fairness)

Zero-shot Interfaith hate recall = 28.2% vs Other hate recall = 1.4% — a 20× gap. Annotator bias from Davidson 2017 AMT workers (predominantly US-based) baked into EN model weights: the model fires on English-pattern hate (transliterated religious terms, international slurs) while being nearly blind to native-Urdu-idiom hate in the "Other" category (n=1,100 test samples).

What would you do differently with more compute or time?
(1) Extend few-shot curve to K=128/256/512 to find where neural catches TF-IDF+LR. (2) Build a Nastaliq-script hate lexicon for effective string-matching features. (3) Run supervised for 3–5 epochs to check for further gains or overfitting. (4) Implement adapter-based fine-tuning for more efficient small-K adaptation. (5) Compute group-conditional FPR using Nastaliq-script lexicons for demographic groups.
How would this system be deployed in practice?
Stage 1: TF-IDF+LR (interpretable, fast, FPR=0.154, no GPU). Stage 2: as labelled Urdu data accumulates, switch to few-shot XLM-R-large. Stage 3: supervised XLM-R-large for maximum recall. Periodically audit Level-2 sub-category recall to catch systematic blind spots. Human review for borderline confidence scores. Prioritise collecting more "Other" category labels since that sub-type has consistently the lowest recall across all models.
Is this methodology generalisable to other low-resource languages?
Yes — with a critical condition: the target language must be in XLM-R's pre-training corpus. For languages in XLM-R's 100-language set, the few-shot stability finding likely generalises. The class-weight calibration lesson (use target-language hate prevalence, not source-language prevalence) applies universally. The cultural domain gap framing is most applicable to languages where target-language hate speech has distinct cultural referents not present in English training data.

Master Q&A — Likely Examiner Questions

Know these cold.

What is SentencePiece BPE and why does it matter for Nastaliq Urdu?
SentencePiece Byte-Pair Encoding starts with characters and iteratively merges the most frequent adjacent pairs into subword tokens. With a 250K vocabulary trained on CommonCrawl, it builds subword pieces spanning 100 languages including Nastaliq Urdu. Since Nastaliq is right-to-left Arabic script, SentencePiece handles it correctly because it operates on Unicode character sequences, not ASCII bytes. Urdu words are split into recognisable subwords carrying semantic meaning, giving XLM-R genuine Urdu representations — unlike mBERT's WordPiece which handles Urdu poorly with its smaller 119K vocabulary.
Why does XLM-R-large beat mBERT at zero-shot Urdu?
Three reasons: (1) 250K vs 119K vocabulary — more Nastaliq Urdu subwords are in-vocabulary, producing meaningful tokens rather than [UNK] fallbacks. (2) CommonCrawl web data contains more informal Urdu than Wikipedia. (3) Temperature-based language sampling (T=0.7) in XLM-R's training upsamples Urdu by ~3–5× vs naive proportional sampling. The capacity ranking on English (XLM-R-large > XLM-R-base > mBERT) is preserved on zero-shot Urdu, confirming Urdu is genuinely represented in the model's weights.
How do you know the zero-shot failure is class-imbalance prior and not domain gap?
By examining the failure mode: (1) Precision = 0.885 — when the model predicts hate, it is almost always correct. (2) Recall = 0.080 — the model almost never predicts hate. If it were a domain gap (model cannot recognise Urdu hate at all), we would expect random firing — low precision AND low recall. Low recall with high precision is the signature of a calibration problem: the decision threshold is set too high. This is consistent with the 94.8% non-hate prior from English training, not language incompatibility.
Why use macro F1 as the primary metric?
A model that never predicts hate gets ~60% accuracy on ISE-Hate but catches zero hate speech. Macro F1 averages F1 across both classes — a degenerate "never fire" model gets F1_hate ≈ 0, F1_nonhate ≈ 0.75, macro F1 ≈ 0.375, barely above random. This correctly penalises the degenerate strategy. Hate F1 is also reported as the primary task metric because it directly measures whether we actually catch hate speech.
What is temperature-based language sampling in XLM-R pre-training?
Without adjustment, training data would be sampled proportional to corpus size — English dominates CommonCrawl at ~50%, leaving Urdu with tiny representation. Temperature sampling with T=0.7 samples language l proportional to p(l)^(1/T): this gives low-resource languages like Urdu ~3–5× more training examples than naive sampling, significantly improving their model representation and therefore zero-shot cross-lingual transfer quality.
What does the K=16 macro F1 instability tell us?
K=16 macro F1 = 0.544 ± 0.135 — high variance. The FPR at K=16 is 0.252, and for some seeds the model over-predicts hate aggressively (FPR > 0.6). With only 16 examples, which 16 are sampled determines whether training is stable: a batch heavy on hate examples pushes the model toward over-predicting hate. By K=32, stability improves (macro F1: 0.676 ± 0.040), and by K=64, variance collapses to ±0.001. Conclusion: ≥32 examples are needed for reliable adaptation; 64 is the effective stable convergence point for ISE-Hate.
What is annotator bias and how does it affect this project?
The Davidson et al. (2017) corpus was labelled by Amazon Mechanical Turk workers — research has shown AMT pools are predominantly white and US-based. These annotators over-labelled African American English (AAE) dialect features as hate speech and were more sensitive to religious-adjacent language familiar to a Western audience. This bias is baked into the English model's weights. When transferred to Urdu, the model fires on content that superficially resembles English hate patterns (transliterated religious terms, internationally used slurs) while missing culturally specific Pakistani hate idiom — causing the 20× zero-shot recall disparity between Interfaith and Other categories.
